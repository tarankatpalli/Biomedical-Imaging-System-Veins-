# Non-Contact Vascular Visualization & Hemodynamic Analysis Using NIR Imaging, 3D Modeling, and Local Edge AI
I designed a low-cost biomedical imaging system that visualizes veins and analyzes blood flow without skin contact while also simultaneously creating a 3D anatomical model of the user's hand using computer vision and edge AI

Blood vessels carry blood throughout the body. When a blood vessel becomes narrowed or blocked, blood flow can decrease and lead to serious health problems. Doctors usually detect these problems using imaging tools like ultrasound or angiograms. However, these methods have limitations, are expensive, require direct contact with the body, and are not always available everywhere. For example, a medical ultrasound machine can cost between $20,000 and $75,000. Because of these limitations, I wanted to explore a low-cost, non-contact alternative. My research focused on how near-infrared light (NIR light) interacts with the human body (specifically veins) help in vein detection and can lead to future research in detecting and preventing venous diseases/abnormalities. 

Veins carry deoxygenated blood which contain an iron-rich protein called hemoglobin, which can absorb infrared light. When the skin is illuminated with NIR light, veins appear darker than the surrounding tissue and can be captured using an infrared-sensitive camera. I also studied remote photoplethysmography, or rPPG. This is a method that measures heart rate without touching the body by detecting tiny changes in light caused by blood pulsing through veins. This allowed our model to do even more like calculating Pulse Wave Velocity (PWV) and Pulse Transit Time (PTT). Another area we explored was 3D modeling. Our goal was to convert 2D hand images into a 3D hand model that shows the detected veins in real time so doctors and researchers could have a spatial understanding of the vein locations and blood flow. Through this interpretation, I explored 3D edge-preserving techniques and camera manipulations to create a 3D hand model. Finally, I researched how computer vision and artificial intelligence can improve vein visibility and its effectiveness to detect changes in circulation.

To test this idea, I designed both a hardware system and a simulation model so I could safely and accurately study blood flow. For hardware, I used a Raspberry Pi, a NoIR camera, and 11 evenly spaced 850-nanometer infrared LEDs arranged in a circular ring to provide equal illumination. The LEDs were powered by a battery and connected using copper wires and a switch, allowing me to control when the system was active. I then placed the entire setup inside a dark enclosure to eliminate visible light, which helped reduce noise and improve measurement accuracy by ensuring the camera only detected infrared light. To safely test blood flow changes, I built a simulated circulatory model using clear silicone tubing filled with red-dyed liquid. This model allowed us to test three controlled conditions:

• Normal flow, where the liquid moved freely

• Partial blockage, created using a small binder clip to reduce flow (simulating tension)

• Full blockage, created using a stronger clamp to stop flow completely 

As the flow decreased, the signal amplitude and measured BPM also decreased, which helped me understand how restricted blood flow aﬀects rPPG signals without any risk to human subjects. I also tested our system on a human arm, including myself, and compared the heart rate results to a medical-grade pulse oximeter (PPG sensor) to validate accuracy and ensure the results were realistic. During testing, I controlled variables such as camera exposure, white balance, distance from the camera, and hand position to reduce experimental error. Each test was repeated multiple times, and I recorded changes in heart rate and signal strength under normal, partial, and fully blocked conditions to confirm consistent results.

For the software portion of this projcet, I developed a Python-based software system to process the infrared video from the NoIR camera in real-time. First, I used Imaging techniques such as Grayscale conversion, CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization), and Image Contours to make veins more easier to see. Since I was using a NoIR camera, these steps helped reduce noise and improve visibility. To make the veins even clearer, I implemented a hybrid approach consisting of a Frangi vesselness filter followed by a custom Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), trained on over 1,500 system-specific infrared images that was designed specifically for the hardware setup. This made the veins significantly clearer than raw footage. Next, I created a 3D anatomical hand model to map vein locations in three-dimensional space. This was mainly achieved using an already predefined Statistical Shape Model (SSM) called [MANO (Hand Model with Articulated and Non-rigid deformations)](https://mano.is.tue.mpg.de/). This model was then enhanced using voxel downsampling and a statistical outlier remover that then depicted the detected veins were projected onto the 3D hand mesh. At the same time, a selected a Region Of Interest (ROI) on the hand was used to extract the average pixel intensity/brightness over time. This signal was filtered to isolate heart rate frequencies, allowing us to calculate beats per minute using rPPG. I also measured Pulse Transit Time (PTT) by tracking how long it took the pulse signal to travel between two points. Using this and the distance between points, I calculated Pulse Wave Velocity (PWV) to find out the speed of the blood in meters per second. To further test this, as blood flow was restricted (in our simulated circulatory model), both the signal strength and pulse wave velocity decreased. Our system's heart rate measurements were about 90% similar compared to a medical pulse-oximeter in terms of accuracy. These results showed that our system could clearly detect changes in blood flow. 

Finally, all data is processed locally using an edge AI model (utilizing a combination of [Ollama](https://ollama.com/) and [Phi3.5](https://ollama.com/library/phi3.5)) and converted all the data into an easy-to-read summary, and was updated every 10 seconds. The purpose of this addition in the project was just to summarize what was happening in the entire code (since it can be very confusing when originally looking at it). Since nothing was sent to the internet, this ensured full data privacy.
